The paper assumes that temperature-related deaths and COVID-19 deaths are independent and simply adds them together to estimate compound mortality impacts. However, extensive literature demonstrates that vulnerable populations for heat/cold mortality and COVID-19 mortality substantially overlap (elderly, those with chronic conditions), which violates this independence assumption and may lead to either double-counting or underestimation of compound effects.

"By adding temperature-related deaths (with COVID-19 deaths removed prior to calculation) and COVID-19 deaths together to estimate the compound mortality impact, we have assumed that they are independent of each other."
The paper explicitly acknowledges this assumption but does not adequately address its implications for the validity of the compound mortality estimates. Simply removing COVID-19 deaths from the mortality data before fitting the temperature-mortality model does not account for potential effect modification, where COVID-19 may have amplified temperature-mortality relationships through shared vulnerabilities, behavioral changes, or healthcare system strain. The approach of treating these as additive effects may misrepresent the true compound risk.
"Because of the common risk factors for temperature-related and COVID-19 mortality, disentangling their effects on health and attributing mortality to a single cause is challenging."
The paper acknowledges this challenge in the discussion but proceeds with the additive assumption nonetheless. This creates a fundamental tension between the methodology used and what the authors recognize as a limitation. Recent studies using interaction models have found that heat-related mortality was significantly elevated during COVID-19 (RR=1.55) compared to pre-pandemic periods (RR=1.07), suggesting the relationship is not simply additive but involves effect modification.
"Heat–pathogen interactions likely influence COVID-19 mortality, as vulnerable populations overlap: the elderly, chronically ill, and those requiring care" [[1]](#citation-list)
This recent study (Ali et al., 2025) in Nature Scientific Reports explicitly quantified the interaction between heat and COVID-19 on mortality using a time-stratified case-crossover design with interaction terms. They found a significant synergistic effect where the risk of heat-related mortality increased with COVID-19 intensity (RR reaching 3.57 during high COVID-19 incidence). This methodological approach directly models the interaction rather than assuming independence, representing a more appropriate analytical framework for compound hazard assessment.
