This paper’s headline conclusions about extreme data efficiency and superiority over prior ML interatomic potentials are not robust because key comparisons (notably the “1000× fewer data” DeepMD comparison) are evaluated on highly correlated MD trajectory frames with train/test splits that can leak near-duplicate configurations, undermining the validity of the reported “orders-of-magnitude” gains.

"NequIP outperforms existing models with up to three orders of magnitude fewer training data, challenging the widely held belief that deep neural networks require massive training sets." 
This is the central, strongest quantitative claim in the abstract and it materially affects the paper’s conclusions about data efficiency. Because it is a comparative statement (“outperforms existing models”), it requires a tightly controlled evaluation protocol that rules out confounders. In MD-generated datasets, adjacent frames can be extremely similar, so “randomly sampled” training frames can have near-neighbors in the test set even if they are formally disjoint. If such leakage occurs, test errors can substantially underestimate true generalization error for deployment (e.g., new thermodynamic conditions or different trajectories). The paper does not establish that its orders-of-magnitude comparisons are robust to these well-known correlation/leakage effects.
"To assess data efficiency of the NequIP architecture, we similarly train a model jointly on all four parts of the data set, but using only 133 structures for training, i.e. 1000x fewer data. The 133 structures were sampled uniformly from the full data set available online, consisting of water and ice structures, made up of a total of 140,000 frames, coming from the same MD trajectories that were used in the earlier work [15]. We also use a validation set of 50 frames and report the test accuracy on all remaining structures in the data set. ... We find that with 1000x fewer training data, NequIP significantly outperforms DeepMD on all four parts of the data set." 
The protocol explicitly samples training/validation frames from the same MD trajectories and then evaluates on “all remaining” frames, which is exactly the situation where temporal correlation can make held-out frames very similar to training frames. That similarity can make interpolation look like strong generalization, especially when the test set contains many near-duplicates of training points. The DeepMD numbers are attributed to a model “previously trained [15]”, but the paper does not specify whether DeepMD was evaluated on exactly the same held-out frames and with the same metric definition, leaving comparability unclear. Since the conclusion hinges on a 1000× data reduction, even modest evaluation mismatches or leakage can qualitatively change the interpretation. As written, the evidence does not justify that the reported 1000× advantage reflects a robust, apples-to-apples generalization improvement.
"However, if the data has many similar conditions, e.g., as occurs for data generated from AIMD trajectories or small perturbations to existing structures, then these random CV approaches will yield overly optimistic predictions. The predictions will be overly optimistic due to the “twin” problem, where extremely similar data is present in both the train and test sets, and the model predictions are thus indicative of data that looks just like the training data." ([1](#citation-list))
This directly describes the failure mode of using random splits on trajectory data: even without explicit leakage, near-duplicate configurations across train/test can yield overly optimistic errors. The paper’s water/ice comparison and several other experiments explicitly sample “uniformly” from MD frames and evaluate on the remaining frames from the same trajectories, matching the scenario warned about here. Because the paper’s main scientific claim is exceptional data efficiency, any optimism bias in the evaluation can inflate the apparent sample-efficiency advantage. The quoted guidance also clarifies what more robust alternatives look like (e.g., testing on new conditions or later trajectory segments), which the paper does not consistently implement for its headline “orders-of-magnitude” comparisons. Therefore, this literature evidence supports that the reported gains may not reflect robust out-of-sample performance.
"We show that the proposed scheme provides an efficient and accurate protocol in a variety of systems, including bulk materials and molecules. In all these cases, DPMD gives results that are essentially indistinguishable from the original data, at a cost that scales linearly with system size." ([4](#citation-list))
This highlights that DeepMD (the principal baseline used for the 1000× claim) is itself presented in the literature as an accurate, symmetry-preserving approach trained on ab initio labels. That makes the paper’s claim of “significantly outperforms DeepMD” with 1000× less data unusually strong, and therefore it warrants especially careful, transparent, and matched evaluation. When a baseline is described as reproducing “results ... essentially indistinguishable from the original data,” modest differences in dataset composition, splitting, and metric definitions can dominate apparent gaps. The paper does not provide enough detail to verify that the DeepMD and NequIP results were produced under identical test conditions. As a result, the conclusion that NequIP fundamentally changes the data requirement relative to DeepMD is not convincingly supported.
